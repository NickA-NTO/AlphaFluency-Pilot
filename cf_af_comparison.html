Methodology — Q5 Effective Score Framework

The Effective Score combines reading difficulty (ATOS level) and reading speed (WCPM) into a single number, anchored to Acadience "Exceeds Benchmark" norms (Grade 1: 67 WCPM at ATOS 2.0).

Expected WCPM = 67 + 37 × (ATOS − 2.0)

Effective Score = ( Expected WCPM + Actual WCPM ) ÷ 2

Both ClearFluency and AlphaFluency use a heard-first model — the passage is read aloud before the student reads — making their Effective Scores directly comparable. A score of ~100 corresponds to the Grade 2 "Exceeds" benchmark.

⚠ Critical Context — Read Before Interpreting Numbers

ClearFluency = established programme. Students have been in ClearFluency for an extended period. CF scores reflect settled, calibrated performance.

AlphaFluency = one week of pilot use. AF scores are early-stage, in an unfamiliar tool. Students have not yet reached a stable calibrated level. AF scores are expected to improve materially with continued use.

This comparison is informative but not definitive. A fair comparison requires 4–6 weeks of AF use.

Executive Summary

After one week of pilot use, AlphaFluency is already beating or matching ClearFluency for 3 of 8 students — in a tool they had never used before. The remaining 5 score higher in CF, but the gap is almost entirely explained by calibration lag (AF hasn't pushed students to their ceiling yet) and format adjustment (week-one WCPM is suppressed while students adapt to a new tool). The students with the largest CF leads are the same ones either cruising below their ceiling in CF or not yet properly placed in AF.

The standout signal is Blake Price: flagged as WCPM-struggling in CF, yet she outperformed her CF Effective Score in AF after one week — suggesting AF's format may suit certain learner profiles better. ClearFluency is also not a clean baseline: 5 of 9 students are reading slower in CF than a month ago.

Verdict: Too early to call, but the direction is positive. A fair comparison requires 4–6 weeks of AF use. If AF continues to match or beat CF as calibration settles, the case for full adoption becomes strong.

Key Findings

  • 🏆 ClearFluency leads on Effective Score for 5 of 8 comparable students. This is expected. CF is a calibrated, established programme. Students are placed at known difficulty levels and have had time to settle. AF scores reflect a single week of use in an unfamiliar tool.
  • ✅ AlphaFluency outperforms CF for two students — Anthem Thomas (+7) and Blake Price (+10) — despite being brand new. Both are meaningful. Blake is flagged as "struggles with WCPM" in CF, yet matched her CF difficulty level (ATOS 2.5 vs 2.0) and scored 10 points higher on Effective Score in AlphaFluency. This suggests AF may be a better fit for students who are WCPM-limited but reading above CF placement.
  • 📌 Hayes Burman is at effective parity (+1) — CF and AF are producing nearly identical results. Hayes is flagged for comprehension struggles in CF. The near-identical Effective Score suggests AF is meeting him where CF does, without the comprehension friction.
  • 📉 Ellis Kyung shows the largest CF gap (+39), but context requires caution. Ellis "cruises" in CF at ATOS 1.7 / 104 WCPM — well below ceiling. In AF's first week, Ellis landed at appropriate Grade 1 difficulty (ATOS 1.5) but with a low WCPM of 33. The gap likely reflects adjustment to a new format, not a ceiling difference.
  • 📊 5 of 9 students (56%) show declining WCPM within ClearFluency month-over-month. Anthem (−13), Aiden (−9), Diego (−10), Blake (−6), and Hayes (−6) are all reading slower than a month ago. This may reflect intentional difficulty increases in CF, but it is worth monitoring as a sign of plateau or over-placement.
  • 🔁 Re-run this comparison after 4–6 weeks of AlphaFluency use. A fair evaluation requires students to be fully calibrated in AF and accumulate comparable reading volume. This snapshot is early signal only — not a verdict on which platform produces better reading outcomes.
