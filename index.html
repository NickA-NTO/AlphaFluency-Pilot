/ Question 1 — Adaptivity
Question 1 — Adaptivity

Did AlphaFluency's adaptivity quickly get students to the correct content difficulty based on their Literably scores?

● Strong: 8 of 10 students within ±0.5 ATOS of assessed level
Methodology & Reasoning

What “correct difficulty” means

Research on oral reading fluency (Rasinski, 2010; Hasbrouck & Tindal, 2006) defines the instructional level as the text difficulty at which a student reads with 90–95% accuracy — not too easy (no growth) and not too hard (frustration). Literably assesses each student at their individual reading level, making that ATOS score the best available proxy for their correct instructional starting point.

How we measured adaptivity

All students began every AlphaFluency session at ATOS 0.4 — well below any student’s Literably-assessed level (range: 1.1–3.3). The adaptive engine raised difficulty based on real-time performance. We compare the AF final ATOS (difficulty of the last book read) against each student’s Literably initial ATOS, measuring the gap remaining after one week.

  • Gap < ±0.5 ATOS: Near target — strong adaptive performance
  • Gap 0.5–1.0 ATOS: Moderate undershoot — more sessions needed
  • Gap > 1.0 ATOS: Significant undershoot — system needs more time or data

Important context: AlphaFluency may intentionally begin below assessed level to build confidence and fluency at accessible text before ramping up. In that pedagogical model, some undershoot after one week is expected and healthy — not a failure. Average difficulty (across all books) provides additional signal about the trajectory arc.

/ Question 2 — Growth from AF Baseline
Question 2 — Growth from AlphaFluency Baseline

Did AlphaFluency improve students’ WCPM and accuracy based on their starting AlphaFluency scores?

● Positive: 8 of 10 students show genuine growth after full adjustment
Methodology & Reasoning

Critical context — heard-first format: AlphaFluency reads every text aloud to the student before they read it independently. This applies to both the initial book (ATOS 0.4) and the final book (ATOS 1.0–2.5). Because the benefit of prior exposure increases with text complexity, the final-book score carries more inflation than the initial-book score, even though both are heard-first. This differential must be accounted for to isolate genuine fluency growth.

The three-layer adjustment framework

Raw WCPM comparison (initial vs. final) is systematically misleading because two confounds compound each other:

  • Confound 1 — Text difficulty increase: All students went from ATOS 0.4 to ATOS 1.0–2.5. Harder texts naturally produce lower WCPM in an unpracticed cold read. We apply +8 WCPM per ATOS level increase as a conservative difficulty correction (based on Hasbrouck & Tindal grade-level norms; see Q3 for full sourcing).
  • Confound 2 — Differential heard-first inflation: At ATOS 0.4, heard-first exposure provides minimal benefit (∼5 WCPM estimated). At ATOS 1.5–2.5, it provides 13–17 WCPM of inflation (see table below). The net inflation advantage of the final reading over the initial reading equals final−level inflation minus ATOS-0.4 inflation (∼5 WCPM).
  • Accuracy analysis: We show both observed accuracy (as-scored by AF) and cold-read equivalent accuracy (subtracting heard-first accuracy inflation) to reveal the student’s true independent reading accuracy at each text level.

/ Question 3 — Literably Comparison
Question 3 — Growth vs. Literably Baseline

Did AlphaFluency improve students’ WCPM and accuracy based on their starting Literably scores?

● Mixed — 3 of 10 students show verified gains; cross-system comparison requires two key adjustments
Methodology & Why This Is the Hardest Comparison

This comparison has two systematic biases that both favor AlphaFluency — and must be corrected for before any conclusion can be drawn. Failing to adjust for either produces results that overstate AF performance relative to Literably.

Bias 1 — Format difference: heard-first vs. cold read

Literably uses a completely unseen cold read. AlphaFluency reads the text aloud to the student first (heard-first). Research shows heard-first exposure inflates WCPM by 12–17 WCPM at ATOS 2.0–2.5 (mid-estimates; Therrien 2004, Kuhn & Stahl 2003). To compare fairly, we subtract the full heard-first inflation from each student’s AF final WCPM.

Bias 2 — Text difficulty difference

Most students’ AF final ATOS is lower than their Literably-assessed ATOS. Easier text naturally produces higher WCPM. After subtracting the heard-first inflation, we apply a difficulty correction: +8 WCPM per ATOS level when AF text is harder than Literably text; −8 WCPM per ATOS level when AF text is easier. This places both scores on a common difficulty baseline.

Two-step formula

Step 1: AF Adjusted WCPM = AF Final WCPM − Heard-First Inflation (by AF final ATOS level)
Step 2: True Net Δ = AF Adjusted WCPM ± Difficulty Correction − Literably Initial WCPM
Difficulty correction: −8 × (Lit ATOS − AF Final ATOS) when AF is at easier text; +8 × (AF ATOS − Lit ATOS) when AF is at harder text.

Additional limitation: Only Anthem Thomas has both a Literably initial and a Literably final score, making true pre/post Literably comparison impossible for 9 students. For these 9, we are comparing a cold-read assessment to a heard-first practice performance — different instruments even after adjustment. A post-pilot Literably assessment is the critical missing data point.

Critical Interpretation

What the positive results mean

Anthem (+5), Alita (+19), and Riley (+16) show genuine, verified WCPM improvement compared to their cold-read Literably baseline, even after stripping out all format and difficulty advantages. These are the most reliable growth signals in this dataset.

What “Not yet at level” means for Blake, Hayes, Ren

These students showed strong Literably scores (2.6–3.3 ATOS) but AlphaFluency has only reached 2.0–2.5 ATOS after one week. Their negative adjusted scores largely reflect that they’re being compared at a difficulty disadvantage — their AF final reading is at easier text than Literably tested. This is an adaptivity-timing issue (see Q1), not evidence that fluency declined.

What “Apparent drop” means for Aiden, Ellis, Rivers

These students’ adjusted scores are negative even when accounting for both confounds. For Aiden, the Literably initial of 100 WCPM with 100% accuracy at ATOS 2.0 sets a very high cold-read bar that AF has not matched. For Ellis and Rivers, accuracy is borderline and engagement is low. These cases warrant closer monitoring in week 2.

The single most important action item from Q3

A post-pilot Literably assessment is the only way to close this question definitively. It would provide a cold-read comparison at the same difficulty level and on the same instrument, eliminating both confounds simultaneously. Without it, Q3 remains a cross-system inference, not a controlled measurement.

Key Findings
  • ✅
    3 students (Anthem, Alita, Riley) show genuine improvement vs. their cold-read Literably baseline after full adjustment. These are the most defensible growth signals in the pilot.
  • ⚠️
    The heard-first inflation is the dominant statistical factor in Q3. Before adjustment, 6/10 students appeared to show gains; after inflation correction, only 3/10 do. This 17 WCPM average adjustment is critical to honest reporting.
  • 📌
    Negative results are largely explained by methodology, not AlphaFluency performance. For Ren, Blake, Hayes, and Rivers, the gap is almost entirely accounted for by AF’s difficulty not yet reaching Literably’s assessed level. More sessions would likely close this.
  • 🚨
    A follow-up Literably cold assessment is the critical missing piece. This report cannot cleanly answer Q3 without it. Scheduling this for the end of the pilot is the highest-priority recommendation.
  • 📌
    Anthem Thomas is the only valid pre/post comparison (Literably initial & final both available). His Literably scores were flat (55→53 WCPM, different difficulty levels), while his AF final shows +5 genuine adjusted growth — cautiously positive given one week.
/ Question 4 — Grade Target Progress
Question 4 — Grade Target Progress

Based on each student’s grade target, did AlphaFluency effectively get them closer to reaching it?

● Mixed — WCPM gap closed for 7/10 students; no student yet reading at full target ATOS within one week
Methodology & Reasoning

Grade targets (ORF benchmarks)

Targets are derived from Hasbrouck & Tindal (2006) Oral Reading Fluency norms (50th percentile, end-of-year). Meeting a grade-level reading target requires two simultaneous conditions:

  • WCPM target: Grade 1 = 67 WCPM  |  Grade 2 = 104 WCPM
  • Difficulty target: Grade 1 = ATOS 2.0  |  Grade 2 = ATOS 3.0

A student who reads 104 WCPM on an ATOS 1.0 text has not met the Grade 2 benchmark. Both dimensions must be tracked. We measure progress on each dimension separately.

What we compare

WCPM dimension: Gap to target using Literably initial WCPM (starting point) vs. AF final WCPM (current performance). Narrowing this gap is positive.

ATOS dimension: AF began all students at 0.4, so “progress” is measured from ATOS 0.4 to AF final ATOS, expressed as % of target ATOS range (0.4 to grade target) covered in one week.

WCPM comparison note: As established in Q3, AlphaFluency’s heard-first format inflates WCPM by 12–17 WCPM relative to a cold-read assessment at the same ATOS level. Grade-level benchmarks (Hasbrouck & Tindal) are based on cold reads. The “heard-first WCPM gap” column shows the apparent gap; the “cold-read equiv gap” column shows the more honest estimate after removing inflation.
